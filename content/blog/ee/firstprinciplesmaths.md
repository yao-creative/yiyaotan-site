Some of my theories and formalizations of what linguistic computing from business people tries to approximate. Notes here were jotted down as unorganized thoughts, so bear with me.

1. Doing things which don't scale/ Good startup ideas: are startup ideas which have high uncertainty for others; however, they have low uncertainty but a high probability of success for founders based on their knowledge. Not high uncertainty and low probability, because that's just absolutely unlikely. Innovation actually happens in fractal branching processes, where a vibe-coding frontend can emerge from combining the known need of website builders with control of technical infrastructure for future scale.

2. Persistence/ grit matters as much as raw talent.

    - External processes are often uncontrollable, where timing and meeting the market at the correct time and place are important. Persistence creates exposure to this luck.

    - Additionally, the value of a company is a multiplicative random process over time. Under the null assumption, the multiplicative distribution of each random roll becomes log-normal.

    - Finally, when one adds the fact that sometimes size just matters in the sense that reputation/ reliability/ trust/ customer-to-customer marketing is like a gravitational and self-reinforcing multiplicative process, which often takes a super-linear effect on the quality of the product one has. Over time, this creates long-tail power laws, as all of these multiplicative effects take time and continuous exposure to luck, in the sense that a random roll goes viral or takes a life of its own in some branching process.

3. A startup is a process rather than an idea / product/ one-hit wonder. Similar to the above, the value of a company is also the aggregate value over time of a self-involutive process integral of t $f_{t+1}(f_{t}(x,t))$.

4. Customers are king: The only real measurable metric of the value a company brings to society is the value of transactions between the company and its customers, which is upper-bounded by the value the company brings to the customer. Fuzzy value can be approximated by revealed preference theory.

5. Tech debt can have heavy effects on iteration speed (Stripe research): Knowing what you don't know is an NP-hard problem similar to [Compression Sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compressed_sensing), where one tries to approximate a higher vector space with a lower-dimensional vector. Figuring out what one doesn't know takes exponential time (approximately, because one can also read online); however, given a higher-dimensional vector, projection is polynomial time. A good hire in the moment lowers the uncertainty around what the company doesn't know. (Which also leads to the value of learning as an individual).


6. Don't focus on pricing. Precise value-ratio pricing is impossible. First of all, if one really wanted to do it, they'd have to propagate the value the company brings to the entire supply chain, which is intractable and has arbitrary-precision versus compute-time trade-offs, not to mention Lyapunov exponents and the limits of time predictability. The best case is that the resource was monopolized on the platform end. However, being a horizontal platform, empowering customers actually creates branching power-law processes for each of the individual downstream B2Bs (value is propagated across the whole supply chain in multipliers), which in turn creates higher alpha coefficients for the power law of value chains starting from your business, rather than playing zero-sum pricing games. [How Supply Chains Amplify Growth - Harvard Growth Lab](www.pnas.org/content/119/1/e2106031118/tab-article-info)

7. Founders matter: Founders are the universal conditioning variable for a startup from start to finish. Beyond that, they are the ones who know exactly how the company functions best by the pure definition of their starting all of the initial operations of the company.

8. Founder-Market fit: One can say that decision-making ability is a function of uncertainty, and the higher the mutual information or the lower the KL divergence between all of the founder's knowledge and the business operations, the better and faster decisions can be made. This, in a sense, is the comparative advantage of founder-led companies and also often why copying other startups or competitors is hard, because the source node of dependency variables is different. The problem for tech startups is not between technical and non-technical; however, it is the "Comparative Advantage of lower Uncertainty."

9. Focus on learning: Due to the reasons in 1, 5, and 9 above, the goal of getting to a good idea is to find a domain which will exist in 20 years and walk towards that edge, gain comparative advantage, and ride power-law process dynamics. Productivity is a super-linear function of knowledge (exponential if you count the ability for ideation and innovation combinations). PMF is just the bifurcation when the activation energy (value of a product to the customer > cost to the customer of using it, including price, time, and alternatives in a fuzzy sense).

10. Moving Fast: is the act of lowering uncertainty as quickly as possible, which leads to better concrete information about outcomes due to the limits of predictability and the decisions made. It is not an ultimate posterior goal.

11. The value of technology is at the frontier: Due to utilitarian pricing functions, the comparative advantage of the service provided is the strongest objective pricing function. Secondly, in each of these technological bifurcations, demand drives price decreases in the previous technological layer: e.g., Moore's law for Microsoft building software and the drop in LLM token prices for AI startups, such that the higher-order layers have exponential gains in profit margins. Additionally, each upstream supply-chain automation and/or productivity increase is a multiplicative effect on price drops, leading to increasing margins.
